# Legal Argumentation Framework

## 1. Types of argument and their audiences

Different forms of legal argument require different structures, levels of technicality, and supporting materials. For the purposes of NormTrace, the following distinctions are useful.

### For strategic litigation and domestic judicial review

- Requires: binding case law where available, precise constitutional and statutory provisions, and where relevant the identification of binding or persuasive interpretive materials.
- Structure: normative premise → subsumption → legal conclusion.
- Language: formal legal-technical language.
- Typical pathways: constitutional review, rights-based judicial review, treaty-based judicial argument where applicable, or other domestic remedial routes depending on jurisdiction.

### For communications before UN bodies or other international mechanisms

- Requires: evidence of exhaustion of domestic remedies, or an explanation of why an exception applies; identification of the state; and specification of the treaty provisions allegedly violated.
- Structure: facts → violated norm → harm → request.
- Language: technical but more accessible, with greater emphasis on concrete human impact.
- Typical mechanisms: individual communications under optional protocols, alternative reports, shadow reports, follow-up submissions, and other supervisory procedures depending on the instrument.

### For legislative advocacy and institutional reform

- Requires: legal argument, practical impact, proposed wording, and where possible some indication of operational or budgetary implications.
- Structure: diagnosis → comparative or contextual evidence → concrete proposal → institutional or political justification.
- Language: accessible to non-specialist legislators, public officials, and policy actors.
- Typical mechanisms: legislative initiatives, parliamentary hearings, committee submissions, expert opinions, consultation processes, and amicus-type interventions where relevant.

### For public advocacy and civil society use

- Requires: translation of legal reasoning into accessible language, together with a clear account of why the issue matters in practice.
- Structure: lived reality or concrete problem → systemic legal issue → possible solution → call to action.
- Language: clear and direct, with minimal jargon.
- Typical mechanisms: civil society reports, statements, campaigns, public briefs, and advocacy materials.

### Relevance for NormTrace

The same analytical finding may need to be reformulated differently depending on the forum. A legally robust argument is not only one that is doctrinally correct, but one that is appropriately structured for its intended audience.

## 2. Structure of a strong legal argument

For NormTrace, the IRAC model can be adapted to normative analysis as follows.

### I — Issue

Identify the legal problem with precision.

The issue should not be framed vaguely as “the law is deficient”, but more specifically, for example:

> “The domestic legal instrument omits an enforceability mechanism for right X, which limits practical access for subgroup Y.”

### R — Rule

Identify the applicable norm or set of norms:

- **Primary norm**: relevant constitutional provision or article of the relevant international legal instrument.
- **Interpretive norm**: General Comment, case law, concluding observation, or other relevant interpretive material.
- **Applicable principles**: *pro persona*, progressivity, non-discrimination, non-regression, equality, accessibility, participation, autonomy, or other relevant principles.

### A — Application

Explain how the applicable rule applies to the domestic legal instrument under review.

Key questions include:

- How does the relevant international standard apply to the domestic provision?
- What legal effect does the challenged or analysed provision actually produce?
- What institutional, practical, or distributive consequences follow from that legal design?
- What empirical, comparative, or contextual material, if any, supports the argument about impact?

### C — Conclusion

State the conclusion clearly and specify the remedial implication.

This should include:

- a precise characterisation of the legal deficiency;
- the type of incompatibility, omission, or implementation-oriented gap identified;
- and the specific remedy sought, whether in the form of legislative amendment, interpretive correction, judicial review, accommodation, institutional redesign, or another remedial pathway.

## 3. Matrix for prioritising findings

Not all findings have the same urgency, remediability, or litigation potential. The following matrix may be used to prioritise findings for reform, litigation, or advocacy.

| Finding | Urgency | Remediability | Litigation potential | Estimated impact on rights-holders | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Gap 1] | High / Medium / Low | Constitutional / Legislative / Regulatory / Administrative | High / Medium / Low | [Estimate] | [1–10] |

### Urgency criteria

- **High**: affects a non-derogable right, generates immediate harm, or results in severe exclusion or discrimination.
- **Medium**: affects a right with immediate obligations, such as accessibility, non-discrimination, or protection against regression.
- **Low**: affects a progressively realised right where the breach does not involve immediate or irreparable harm.

### Remediability criteria

- **Constitutional amendment**: requires constitutional reform and is therefore institutionally more demanding.
- **Legislative reform**: requires statutory amendment.
- **Regulatory reform**: can be addressed through executive or delegated rule-making.
- **Administrative measure**: can be addressed through institutional or administrative action without formal legal amendment.

### Litigation potential criteria

- **High**: there is an identifiable affected group, a reasonably stable legal basis, and a plausible pathway to judicial or quasi-judicial review.
- **Medium**: there is a solid legal basis, but the issue remains novel or procedurally difficult.
- **Low**: the argument depends on highly innovative interpretation, uncertain standing, or prior institutional change.

### Relevance for NormTrace

This matrix is not a substitute for legal judgment. It is a heuristic tool for deciding which findings are most strategically relevant in a given institutional setting.

## 4. Reference authors and doctrinal areas

This section identifies thematic reference areas that may support legal argumentation. It is not intended as an exhaustive bibliography, but as a guide to recurring bodies of thought relevant to NormTrace.

### International human rights law

- Héctor Fix-Fierro and Sandra Serrano — state obligations and constitutional rights theory in Mexico.
- Sergio García Ramírez — Inter-American system and conventionality control.
- Cecilia Medina Quiroga — commentary and analysis on the American Convention on Human Rights.
- Manfred Nowak — commentary on the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights.

### Social model of disability and CRPD scholarship

- Agustina Palacios — social model of disability.
- Gerard Quinn and Theresia Degener — human rights and disability.
- Amita Dhanda — legal capacity and Article 12 CRPD.
- Silvia Quan-Chang — women with disabilities in Latin America.

### Gender and law

- Alda Facio — gender perspective in legal analysis and legislative design.
- Martha Nussbaum — capabilities approach and gender.
- Catharine MacKinnon — feminist legal theory.
- Marcela Lagarde — gender violence and structural inequality.

### Intersectionality in legal analysis

- Kimberlé Crenshaw — foundational work on intersectionality.
- Patricia Hill Collins — intersectional power structures and social theory.
- Inter-American Court of Human Rights, *Gonzales Lluy et al. v. Ecuador* (2015) — relevant for intersectional reasoning in the Inter-American context.

### Indigenous rights and legal pluralism

- Rodolfo Stavenhagen — indigenous rights and human rights.
- Raquel Yrigoyen Fajardo — legal pluralism and indigenous law in Latin America.
- James Anaya — indigenous peoples in international law.
- ILO materials on Convention No. 169.

### Legislative drafting and institutional design

- Felipe Tena Ramírez — Mexican constitutional law.
- José María de la Garza — legislative drafting in Mexico.
- UNAM-IIJ materials on legislative technique.
- European Parliament drafting guidance and legislative manuals.

### Analytical note for NormTrace

These references should be used selectively and functionally. Their role is to strengthen the legal and analytical basis of an argument, not to overload the argument with secondary literature where a primary legal source is available.

## 5. Common errors to avoid in legal argumentation

### Errors in analysis

- Do **not** confuse the wording of a domestic legal instrument with its actual legal effect.
- Do **not** argue exclusively from text without examining the broader institutional and systemic context.
- Do **not** assume that naming a right is equivalent to guaranteeing it.
- Do **not** ignore helpful domestic case law merely because an international source exists.

### Errors in drafting

- Do **not** use language that reproduces the discrimination being criticised.
- Do **not** make the argument so technical that it becomes unusable for the intended audience.
- Do **not** propose reform without considering institutional feasibility and budgetary implications where relevant.
- Do **not** rely on secondary sources when the relevant primary source is available.

### Errors in reform proposals

- Do **not** call for repeal without proposing an alternative normative pathway.
- Do **not** propose wording that addresses the gap only formally but not substantively.
- Do **not** ignore transitional provisions, implementation clauses, or institutional follow-through.
- Do **not** overlook the operational and budgetary implications of the proposed reform.

### Analytical note for NormTrace

Where possible, legal argumentation should rely first on:

1. treaty text;
2. constitutional text;
3. binding or authoritative case law;
4. interpretive materials from relevant supervisory bodies;
5. and only then on secondary doctrine.

## 7. Template for structured legal argumentation

The following template may be used for litigation, legislative advocacy, or structured legal analysis.

```text
LEGAL ARGUMENT [NUMBER]

1. CHALLENGED OR ANALYSED DOMESTIC LEGAL INSTRUMENT:
   [Article X of Law Y]

2. RIGHT AFFECTED:
   [Name of the right + article of the relevant constitutional or international legal instrument]

3. CONTENT OF THE APPLICABLE STANDARD:
   [Paraphrase of the relevant norm + interpretive guidance from case law, a General Comment, or another relevant interpretive material]
   Source: [instrument / decision / General Comment / paragraph]

4. WAY IN WHICH THE DOMESTIC LEGAL INSTRUMENT FAILS TO MEET THE STANDARD:
   □ Total absence of the right
   □ Recognition without guarantee
   □ Regression from a prior standard
   □ Direct incompatibility
   □ Indirect discrimination
   □ Omission affecting a specific subgroup
   □ Implementation-oriented deficiency

   Explanation:
   [Concrete analysis]

5. RELEVANT PRECEDENT OR INTERPRETIVE MATERIAL:
   [Case / General Comment / concluding observation / paragraph]

6. LEGAL CONSEQUENCE:
   [Possible domestic unconstitutionality, incompatibility with treaty obligations, discriminatory effect, or basis for international responsibility]

7. POSSIBLE REFORM OR REMEDIAL PATHWAY:
   Current wording: [...]
   Proposed wording: [...]
   Justification: [...]

## 8. Methodological cautions

When using this framework, analysts should take particular care to:
- distinguish between normative alignment, legal compatibility, and broader questions of implementation;
   - match the form of argument to the intended audience and forum;
   - distinguish between a domestic legal framework and a specific domestic legal instrument;
   - keep diagnosis analytically distinct from remedial proposal;
   - and ensure that legal argument is supported by primary legal materials wherever possible.

In NormTrace, legal argumentation is the stage at which analytical findings are translated into a structured form that can be used in litigation, legislative advocacy, public-facing analysis, institutional reform processes, or later comparative work.
