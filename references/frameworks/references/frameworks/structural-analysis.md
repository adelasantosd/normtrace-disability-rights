# Structural Analysis Framework
## Structural Review of Domestic Legal Instruments

This framework sets out the structural method used by NormTrace to analyse the formal design, institutional architecture, and enforceability pathways of a domestic legal instrument.

Its purpose is not merely to describe the internal organisation of a statute or regulation, but to assess whether the domestic legal instrument creates a coherent, enforceable, and rights-compatible institutional structure.

For the purposes of NormTrace, structural analysis is a core component of broader **normative alignment** and **legal compatibility** review. A domestic legal instrument may use rights-based language yet remain structurally weak if it allocates responsibilities poorly, omits institutional mechanisms, or fails to create enforceable obligations.

---

## 1. Legislative drafting: formal elements

### Types of domestic legal instruments in Mexico

| Type of instrument | General position in the legal order | Issuing authority | Basic legal procedure |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Constitution / constitutional amendment** | Highest | Congress of the Union + majority of state legislatures | Article 135 CPEUM |
| **Constitutional law** | Constitutional or constitutionally linked | Congress of the Union | Depending on the constitutional basis |
| **General law** | Federal coordinating statute with nationwide effects within its field | Congress of the Union | Defines or distributes competences across levels of government |
| **Federal law** | Federal statutory level | Congress of the Union | Applies within the federal sphere |
| **Organic law** | Federal or state statutory level | Relevant legislature | Organises powers or public institutions |
| **Regulation** | Subordinate to statute | Executive branch | Article 89(I) CPEUM, where applicable |
| **Official Mexican Standard (NOM)** | Technical-regulatory instrument | Ministries and competent authorities | Technical and administrative procedure |
| **Decree** | Variable depending on subject matter | Executive or legislature | Depends on constitutional or statutory basis |

### Key distinction: General law vs federal law

A **general law** distributes competences among the federation, the states, and the municipalities. It therefore creates a framework within which multiple orders of government may have binding duties and, where appropriate, concurrent legislative or implementation responsibilities.

A **federal law**, by contrast, operates within the federal sphere and is principally directed at federal authorities and institutions unless the constitutional framework provides otherwise.

### Relevance for NormTrace

This distinction matters because the legal effect of a domestic legal instrument cannot be assessed correctly without identifying the level of government it binds and the competences it allocates.

In the field of disability rights, for example, the **LGIPD** is a **general law**. This means that state legislatures are expected to legislate in harmony with its framework, and municipalities may also bear direct implementation obligations depending on the matter concerned.

## 2. Internal anatomy of a Mexican statute

A typical general law in Mexico often follows a structure similar to the following:

```text
PRELIMINARY TITLE / GENERAL PROVISIONS
  ├── Purpose of the law
  ├── Scope of application (personal / material / territorial / temporal)
  ├── Definitions
  └── Guiding principles

TITLE I–N: RIGHTS / POLICY AREAS / SUBJECT MATTERS
  ├── Chapters organised by sector or right (e.g. health, education, work)
  └── Each chapter commonly includes the right, duty, or policy area plus the responsibilities of public authorities

PENULTIMATE TITLE: INSTITUTIONAL STRUCTURE
  ├── Bodies created or designated
  ├── Powers and functions
  └── Inter-institutional coordination arrangements

FINAL TITLE: SANCTIONS AND LIABILITIES
  └── Types of infringement and their consequences

TRANSITIONAL PROVISIONS
  └── Entry into force, repeals, implementation deadlines, and transitional duties
```
**Relevance for NormTrace**

Structural analysis should determine whether this anatomy produces more than formal order. The key question is whether the internal structure of the domestic legal instrument translates rights language into:
- identifiable duties,
- competent institutions,
- coordination pathways,
- enforceability mechanisms,
- and implementation timelines.

## 3. Normative actor mapping: method

The purpose of normative actor mapping is to identify who is legally required to act, who may act, and who remains structurally invisible within the domestic legal instrument.

**Step A. Identify verbs of obligation and their subjects**

Systematically identify obligation-bearing and power-conferring verbs together with their legal subject.

**Positive obligation**
Examples include:
- shall guarantee
- shall ensure
- shall promote
- shall establish
- shall implement
- shall provide

**Duties of abstention**
Examples include:
	•	shall refrain from
	•	is prohibited from
	•	may not

**Discretionary powers**
Examples include:
- may
- is authorised to
- is empowered to

These require caution because discretionary powers are not equivalent to enforceable duties.

**Aspirational or weakly binding language**
Examples include:
- shall seek to
- shall endeavour to
- shall encourage
- shall foster

These formulations may indicate low legal density or weak enforceability.

### Step B. Classify the type of obligation

For rights-based analysis, obligations can often be grouped into three broad categories:
- Respect: duty not to interfere with the exercise of the right.
- Protect: duty to prevent third parties from interfering with the right.
- Fulfil / guarantee: duty to adopt positive measures to make the right effective.

### Step C. Verify the enforceability chain
For each relevant obligation, trace the following sequence:
```
Rights-holder → complaint / claim / request → receiving authority → response mechanism → consequence for non-compliance
```
If one or more links in this chain are absent, inaccessible, or indeterminate, the obligation may be legally weak or merely declaratory.

Relevance for NormTrace

Normative actor mapping is a central tool for identifying:
- whether the domestic legal instrument creates legally meaningful duties;
- whether the relevant authority is clearly identified;
- whether private actors are included where necessary;
- and whether the structure allows rights-holders to move from formal entitlement to practical claimability.

## 4. Analysis of institutional structure
Structural analysis should assess whether the domestic legal instrument creates an institutional architecture capable of giving effect to the right in question.

**Diagnostic questions**
1.	Does the instrument create a new body or assign functions to an existing one?
2.	Does the body have its own budget or depend on reallocation within an existing ministry or authority?
3.	Does it have technical autonomy or is it fully subordinated to a ministry or department?
4.	Can it issue binding decisions or only recommendations?
5.	Is there an intersectoral coordination mechanism with actual legal authority rather than merely consultative or symbolic arrangements?
6.	Does the structure provide for participation by civil society and rights-holders themselves?

**Institutional warning signs**
- Concentration in a single ministry or authority: where all functions are concentrated in one sectoral authority, the design may reproduce a narrow policy model.
- Collegiate body without quorum or mandatory periodicity: the body may become formally existent but practically dormant.
- Lack of a technical secretariat or administrative support: institutions without operational structure often fail in practice.
- Absence of earmarked or identifiable funding: duties without funding may remain aspirational.
- Transitional provisions without deadlines: implementation may be indefinitely postponed.

**Relevance for NormTrace**
A domestic legal instrument may be formally rights-compatible while remaining institutionally fragile. Structural analysis should therefore identify whether the institutional architecture is:
- legally coherent,
- operationally plausible,
- adequately resourced,
- and capable of supporting implementation rather than merely symbolic recognition.

## 5. Medical model vs social model of disability

This framework is especially relevant to disability-rights analysis, but it may also have broader analytical value where domestic legal instruments regulate groups exposed to structural disadvantage: 

| Dimension | Medical / assistentialist model | Social / rights-based model |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| Problem identified | The individual or their condition | Barriers in the social, legal, and physical environment | 
| Subject of the norm | “Beneficiary”, “patient” | Rights-holder | 
| State response | Assistance, treatment, rehabilitation | Reasonable accommodation, universal accessibility, inclusion | 
| Participation | Object of policy | Agent in policy design and implementation | 
| Equality standard | Formal equality | Substantive equality | 
| Typical language | “Invalid”, “incapable”, “special needs” | “Person with disabilities”, “reasonable accommodation” | 
| Policy objective | Normalisation / integration | Inclusion / independent living | 

**Relevance for NormTrace**

This distinction is not merely conceptual. It can shape:
- the definition of the rights-holder,
- the legal duties assigned to public authorities,
- the kinds of remedy created,
- and the institutional location of the policy response.

In structural analysis, the question is whether the domestic legal instrument is built around rights and barriers, or around impairment and assistance.

## 6. Analysis of scope of application

The scope of a domestic legal instrument should be examined across several dimensions.

**Dimensions of scope**
1.	**Personal scope**: To whom does the instrument apply? Are thresholds used that may exclude relevant groups, such as only recognising “permanent” disability?
2.	**Material scope**: Which policy areas or legal domains are covered? Are there sectoral omissions?
3.	**Territorial scope**: Does the instrument operate at federal, state, municipal, or multi-level scale? Does it account for rural and indigenous contexts?
4.	**Temporal scope**: Does it apply immediately? Are there transitional arrangements, acquired-rights issues, or progressive implementation clauses?
5.	**Passive subject scope**: Does the instrument bind only public authorities, or does it also regulate private actors such as employers, private educational institutions, or service providers?

**The passive subject trap**
A domestic legal instrument that binds only the state may leave major forms of rights violation by private actors outside effective regulation.
For many structurally disadvantaged groups, discrimination and exclusion frequently occur in settings involving employers, families, service providers, private schools, landlords, or care institutions. Structural analysis should therefore assess whether the domestic legal instrument addresses only public duties or also private obligations where the right requires it.

**Relevance for NormTrace**
Scope analysis is essential for determining whether an apparently inclusive domestic legal instrument is in fact narrow, partial, or selectively protective.

A norm may appear rights-based in principle while excluding important categories of rights-holders, relevant subject matters, or key duty-bearing actors.

## 7. Methodological cautions

When using this framework, analysts should take particular care to:
- distinguish between a domestic legal framework and a specific domestic legal instrument;
- avoid assuming that a formally well-drafted provision is necessarily enforceable;
- distinguish between binding duties, discretionary powers, and aspirational language;
- analyse institutional architecture together with legal wording;
- treat funding, coordination, and participation as structural elements rather than secondary implementation details;
- and assess whether the legal design produces a workable enforceability chain for rights-holders.

In NormTrace, structural analysis is used to determine whether a domestic legal instrument creates a credible pathway from formal recognition to institutional effect.
