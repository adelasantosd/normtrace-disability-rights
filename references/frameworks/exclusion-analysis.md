# Exclusion and Intersectionality Analysis Framework

## 1. Theoretical foundation: direct, indirect, and structural discrimination

For the purposes of NormTrace, exclusion analysis begins by distinguishing among different forms of discrimination. This distinction is important because a domestic legal instrument may appear formally neutral while still producing exclusionary legal effects.

### Direct discrimination

Direct discrimination occurs where a domestic legal instrument expressly differentiates and disadvantages a person or group on the basis of a protected characteristic.

**Illustrative legislative example:**  
A law that prohibits persons with psychosocial disabilities from exercising the right to vote.

### Indirect discrimination

Indirect discrimination occurs where an apparently neutral rule or requirement produces disproportionately adverse effects for a protected group.

**Illustrative legislative example:**  
A requirement of “oral communication” for access to public services may exclude Deaf persons even where they are not explicitly named.

### Structural discrimination

Structural discrimination occurs where a domestic legal instrument reproduces or consolidates historically unequal social arrangements, even without explicitly intending to do so.

**Illustrative legislative example:**  
A law that assigns the care of persons with disabilities to “the family” may reproduce the feminisation of unpaid care work without naming gender at all.

### Relevance for NormTrace

This distinction is important because exclusion may arise through:

- express legal prohibition;
- apparently neutral drafting with unequal effects;
- or legal design that embeds unequal social assumptions.

NormTrace therefore treats exclusion analysis as part of broader **normative alignment** and **legal compatibility** review.

### Reference points

Relevant legal and interpretive materials include:

- CEDAW Committee, General Recommendation No. 28;
- CRPD, especially Article 3;
- CRPD Committee, General Comment No. 3 on women and girls with disabilities;
- Inter-American Court of Human Rights, Advisory Opinion OC-18/03 on the juridical condition and rights of undocumented migrants, especially for the broader principle of non-discrimination;
- other jurisdictionally relevant equality and non-discrimination standards where applicable.

## 2. Gender perspective: ten diagnostic questions

When analysing a domestic legal instrument, the following questions may help identify whether gender has been incorporated only formally, materially, or not at all.

1. **Is the legal subject gendered?**  
   Does the instrument treat women and men identically where their structural position is not the same?

2. **Does the instrument recognise the intersection between gender and the principal regulated category?**  
   For example: women with disabilities, indigenous women, girls in street situations.

3. **Does the instrument use generic masculine language or inclusive language?**  
   Legal language is not neutral; the choice of terms may shape who is imagined as the legal subject.

4. **Is there any provision specifically addressing women and girls within the regulated group?**  
   The absence of such a provision may amount to exclusion by omission.

5. **Is gender-based violence incorporated as a legally relevant variable?**  
   Women with disabilities, for example, may face elevated exposure to domestic, institutional, and sexual violence.

6. **Are care obligations framed with a gender perspective?**  
   If the domestic legal instrument simply states that “the family” shall be responsible, the practical burden may fall overwhelmingly on women.

7. **Are participation mechanisms genuinely accessible to women?**  
   Accessibility may depend on timing, location, caregiving burdens, language, and cultural constraints.

8. **Are monitoring indicators disaggregated by sex?**  
   Without such disaggregation, inequalities may remain legally invisible.

9. **Does the instrument incorporate substantive equality or only formal equality?**  
   Formal equality treats differently situated persons as if they were similarly placed; substantive equality addresses differences in starting position and structural disadvantage.

10. **Differentiated impact test**  
    Imagine the practical application of each relevant provision to the life of a woman and a man within the regulated group. Would the result be materially the same?

### Relevance for NormTrace

A domestic legal instrument may be formally universal while remaining gender-blind in its institutional design. NormTrace therefore asks whether gender has been incorporated:

- in the definition of the rights-holder;
- in the design of duties and remedies;
- in participation and monitoring;
- and in the recognition of unequal exposure to harm.

### Reference frameworks

Relevant legal and interpretive materials include:

- CEDAW, especially Articles 2, 4, and 5;
- Convention of Belém do Pará where regionally relevant;
- CEDAW Committee, General Recommendation No. 25 on substantive equality;
- CEDAW Committee, General Recommendation No. 35 on gender-based violence;
- CRPD, Article 6;
- and other jurisdictionally relevant gender-equality standards.

## 3. Intersectional analysis: method

Intersectionality, first articulated by Kimberlé Crenshaw and further developed in legal and human rights analysis, recognises that multiple forms of disadvantage do not simply accumulate arithmetically. They interact to produce qualitatively distinct forms of exclusion.

For NormTrace, intersectional analysis is used to assess whether a domestic legal instrument is designed around an abstract, homogeneous legal subject, or whether it acknowledges differentiated conditions of access, risk, and exclusion.

### Axes of intersection to review in any domestic legal instrument

| Axis | Key questions | Relevant reference framework |
|---|---|---|
| **Gender** | Does the instrument differentiate experiences by gender? | CEDAW; CRPD Article 6 |
| **Ethnicity / indigeneity** | Does it recognise cultural and linguistic diversity in access? | ILO Convention No. 169; UNDRIP; jurisdiction-specific constitutional norms where applicable |
| **Age** | Are there specific provisions for children, adolescents, or older persons? | CRC; CRPD Articles 7 and 8 |
| **Class / poverty** | Are services free in law but costly in practice? | ICESCR; equality and social-rights standards |
| **Rurality** | Can the mechanisms operate outside urban centres? | CEDAW General Recommendation No. 34 on rural women; access-to-services standards |
| **Migration status** | Does the instrument apply only to nationals or documented persons? | Equality and non-discrimination standards; migrant-rights instruments where applicable |
| **Sexual orientation / gender identity** | Does the instrument recognise diversity in orientation and identity? | Yogyakarta Principles; evolving regional and domestic standards |
| **Deprivation of liberty** | Does it address the regulated group in detention or institutional settings? | CRPD; Nelson Mandela Rules; other detention-related standards |

### Method for intersectional analysis

1. Identify the rights-holder in the most disadvantaged position within the regulated group.  
   For example: an indigenous woman with disabilities in a rural area without internet access.

2. Trace that person’s route of access to each right or guarantee recognised in the domestic legal instrument.

3. Identify the point at which that route becomes interrupted, inaccessible, or impracticable.

4. Assess whether that interruption is caused by:
   - an omission in the legal instrument;
   - a specific provision;
   - an institutional design failure;
   - or an access barrier built into the legal architecture.

### Relevance for NormTrace

Intersectionality is not treated here as an abstract theoretical add-on. It is used as a diagnostic method for identifying:

- hidden exclusions;
- subgroup-specific implementation-oriented gaps;
- and apparently universal legal designs that are in fact structured around a narrow and privileged legal subject.

## 4. Indigenous perspective and culturally specific exclusion

### Minimum normative framework

Relevant standards may include, depending on jurisdiction:

- **ILO Convention No. 169**: consultation, recognition of indigenous institutions, and access to services in culturally and linguistically appropriate forms;
- **UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP)**;
- relevant constitutional or statutory recognition of indigenous peoples;
- linguistic-rights frameworks where applicable.

### Diagnostic questions

1. Does the domestic legal instrument expressly mention indigenous peoples, or does it assume an urban, dominant-language legal subject?
2. Are there consultation mechanisms in the design, implementation, or revision of the instrument?
3. Are services and procedures accessible in relevant indigenous or minority languages?
4. Does the instrument recognise or ignore indigenous systems of care, justice, or social organisation?
5. Does it address geographic access for remote communities?
6. Do the programmes or mechanisms derived from the instrument require documentation or access conditions that many rights-holders may not possess in practice?

### The trap of exclusionary universalism

Some domestic legal instruments claim to protect “all persons” but design their access pathways around:

- internet access;
- official documentation;
- geographic proximity to urban services;
- and communication in the dominant official language.

In practice, such supposedly universal structures may exclude many rights-holders without naming them explicitly.

### Relevance for NormTrace

NormTrace therefore asks not only whether the domestic legal instrument is formally universal, but whether its design is **socially, culturally, and geographically reachable** for the rights-holders concerned.

### Country-use note

For detailed constitutional and institutional treatment of indigenous or culturally specific rights, analysts should consult the relevant country module in `references/countries/`.

## 5. Language as a technology of exclusion

Legal language is not merely descriptive. It shapes the legal relationship between the state, institutions, private actors, and rights-holders.

For NormTrace, language analysis is important because terminology can convert a rights-based obligation into a discretionary, paternalistic, or assistentialist framework.

### Classification of legal language by effect

#### Objectifying or medicalising language

Examples include:

- “invalid”;
- “incapable”;
- “special needs”;
- “suffers from”;
- “victim of”;
- broad labels that construct persons as passive recipients rather than active rights-holders.

Such language may reinforce an assistentialist or medical model.

#### Rights-based language

Examples include:

- “persons with disabilities”;
- “reasonable accommodation”;
- “universal accessibility”;
- “rights-holders”;
- “barriers”;
- “discrimination”.

Such language is more consistent with a social and rights-based model.

### Legal significance of language

Assistentialist language is not merely a symbolic problem. It can shape the legal relationship as one of discretionary state beneficence rather than enforceable duty.

### Five-point language test

1. Does the domestic legal instrument define persons as active rights-holders or passive recipients?
2. Does it locate the problem in the individual or in the surrounding environment and institutional structure?
3. Does it use terminology consistent with current international standards?
4. Does the language reproduce stereotypes, such as total dependence or incapacity?
5. Is the language accessible, or is it so technical or exclusionary that rights-holders cannot realistically invoke it without intermediaries?

### Relevance for NormTrace

Language analysis helps identify whether a domestic legal instrument is aligned with a rights-based model not only in substance but also in legal construction.

## 6. Normative silence: typology and legal consequences

NormTrace treats normative silence as analytically significant. The absence of regulation, the under-specification of duties, and the deferral of essential content may all have legal consequences.

| Type of silence | Description | Legal effect | Possible challenge or response pathway |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Absolute legislative omission** | The right or guarantee is not regulated at all | Legal vacuum or major under-protection | Constitutional or rights-based challenge; institutional recommendation; legislative reform |
| **Relative omission** | The right is regulated for some but not for the subgroup under analysis | Exclusionary protection; possible indirect or structural discrimination | Equality-based challenge; strategic litigation; treaty-body communication where applicable |
| **Declaratory regulation** | The right is named but no institutional or remedial mechanism is created | Symbolic recognition without practical enforceability | Litigation seeking implementation; demand for regulation or institutional action |
| **Budgetary silence** | The duty exists but no funding or financial pathway is identified | Serious implementation-oriented gap | Budget advocacy; transparency requests; litigation or oversight strategies where applicable |
| **Delegation to regulation without deadline** | The statute states that future regulation will define the matter, but without a clear deadline | Indefinite postponement; legal opacity | Petitioning, judicial challenge where appropriate, legislative amendment, oversight action |

### Relevance for NormTrace

Silence is not neutral. It may:

- make rights difficult to invoke;
- shift burdens to rights-holders and families;
- obscure who is legally responsible;
- and transform a supposedly protective domestic legal instrument into an under-specified framework with weak institutional effect.

---

## 7. Methodological cautions

When using this framework, analysts should take particular care to:

- distinguish between **direct**, **indirect**, and **structural** discrimination;
- identify whether exclusion lies in wording, omission, institutional design, or access pathway;
- distinguish between a formally universal rule and a substantively exclusionary one;
- analyse language, participation, accessibility, and subgroup-specific access as legally relevant design questions;
- use the terms **rights-holders**, **domestic legal instrument**, **international standard**, **normative alignment**, and **implementation-oriented gap** consistently across the repository;
- and keep diagnosis analytically distinct from advocacy or reform proposal.

In NormTrace, exclusion analysis is used to identify whether a domestic legal instrument renders certain rights-holders invisible, conditionally protected, or structurally excluded, even where the instrument appears neutral or inclusive on its face.
