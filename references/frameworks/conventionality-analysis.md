# Conventionality Analysis Framework

## 1. What conventionality control is

Conventionality control refers to the duty of state authorities to ensure that domestic legal instruments, acts, and practices are compatible with applicable international human rights treaties and with the interpretive standards developed by the relevant supervisory bodies.

Within the Inter-American system, the doctrine was articulated by the Inter-American Court of Human Rights in *Almonacid Arellano et al. v. Chile* (2006, para. 124) and further developed in subsequent decisions, including *Dismissed Congressional Employees (Aguado Alfaro et al.) v. Peru* (2006) and *Cabrera García and Montiel Flores v. Mexico* (2010).

For the purposes of NormTrace, conventionality analysis does not mean simple textual comparison between a domestic legal instrument and an international legal instrument. It means assessing whether the **legal effect**, **institutional design**, and **practical enforceability** of a domestic legal instrument are compatible with the applicable **international standard**.

### Relevance in Mexico

In Mexico, Article 1 of the Constitution, as amended in June 2011, provides that human rights norms shall be interpreted in accordance with the Constitution and with the international treaties to which Mexico is party, always favouring the broadest protection for persons.

For methodological purposes, this means that domestic legal analysis must engage with both constitutional and international human rights standards.

## 2. The constitutional parameter of rights review in Mexico

For the purposes of NormTrace, the Mexican constitutional parameter of rights review includes:

1. the **Political Constitution of the United Mexican States (CPEUM)**;
2. **international human rights treaties** ratified by Mexico;
3. relevant jurisprudence of the **Inter-American Court of Human Rights**;
4. interpretive materials such as **General Comments** issued by UN treaty bodies;
5. relevant decisions of the **Supreme Court of Justice of the Nation (SCJN)**.

### Internal hierarchy after the 2011 reform

Following *Contradicción de Tesis 293/2011*, the working position commonly used in Mexican rights adjudication may be summarised as follows:

- where the Constitution does **not** contain an express restriction, constitutional and treaty-based human rights standards are read together within the constitutional parameter, with preference given to the most protective interpretation;
- where the Constitution contains an **express constitutional restriction**, that restriction prevails within the domestic legal order.

### Analytical note for NormTrace

For operational purposes, NormTrace refers to this framework as the **constitutional parameter of rights review**, rather than assuming a flat equivalence among all sources. This distinction matters when analysing **normative alignment** and **legal compatibility**, especially in areas where constitutional text, treaty standards, and judicial doctrine do not align perfectly.

## 4. Method for conventionality analysis

NormTrace applies a five-step method for conventionality analysis.

### Step 1. Identify the right in the relevant international legal instrument

- Locate the relevant treaty provision or provisions.
- Review any General Comments, concluding observations, or interpretive materials relevant to that right.
- Verify whether there is relevant jurisprudence from the Inter-American Court, the SCJN, or other authoritative bodies.

### Step 2. Determine the minimum content of the right

Each right has a minimum or essential content that cannot be emptied without undermining the right itself.

Questions to guide this step include:

- What basic guarantee must exist for the right to be real rather than merely declaratory?
- What does the international legal instrument absolutely prohibit?
- What must the state actively do in order to respect, protect, and fulfil the right?

### Step 3. Identify the corresponding domestic legal instrument or provision

- Does the domestic legal framework contain a corresponding provision?
- If not, is there a legislative omission?
- If it exists, how is it drafted?
- What legal effect does it actually produce in terms of enforceability, access, institutional duty, and remedy?

### Step 4. Analyse the gap

The relevant gap may take different forms:

- total absence;
- weak or declaratory recognition;
- regression;
- direct incompatibility;
- indirect discrimination;
- omission affecting a specific subgroup;
- implementation-oriented deficiency.

The analysis should also determine:

- whether the gap is partial or total;
- whether it affects all rights-holders or specific subgroups;
- whether it can be addressed by regulation or requires legislative amendment.

### Step 5. Identify the legal consequence

The final step is to assess the legal significance of the gap.

Relevant questions include:

- Could the gap engage the state’s international responsibility?
- Could it be challenged domestically through constitutional or rights-based review?
- Could it support an international communication or petition?
- Is there a comparable precedent in another jurisdiction or supervisory body?

## 5. Key interpretive materials by instrument

### CRPD Committee — key General Comments

- **General Comment No. 1 (2014)**: Equal recognition before the law (Article 12) — rejects substituted decision-making and requires supported decision-making.
- **General Comment No. 2 (2014)**: Accessibility (Article 9) — treats accessibility as an immediate obligation and clarifies that it also applies to private actors in relevant settings.
- **General Comment No. 3 (2016)**: Women and girls with disabilities (Articles 6 and 7) — highlights the intersection of gender and disability.
- **General Comment No. 4 (2016)**: Right to inclusive education (Article 24) — rejects segregated special education as a substitute for inclusive education.
- **General Comment No. 5 (2017)**: Living independently and being included in the community (Article 19).
- **General Comment No. 6 (2018)**: Equality and non-discrimination (Article 5) — addresses multiple and intersectional discrimination.
- **General Comment No. 7 (2018)**: Participation of persons with disabilities, including representative organisations.
- **General Comment No. 8 (2022)**: Right to work and employment (Article 27).

### Concluding observations relevant to Mexico

- **Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities**, Concluding observations on the initial report of Mexico (2014) — `CRPD/C/MEX/CO/1`
- **CEDAW Committee**, Concluding observations on Mexico (2018) — `CEDAW/C/MEX/CO/9`
- **Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights**, Concluding observations on Mexico (2018) — `E/C.12/MEX/CO/5-6`

### Analytical note for NormTrace

General Comments and concluding observations do not have the same formal status as treaty text, but they are highly relevant interpretive materials. For methodological purposes, they should be used to clarify the scope, content, and practical implications of treaty obligations.

## 6. Relevant SCJN and Inter-American case law

### Key SCJN references

- **Contradicción de Tesis 293/2011**: foundational decision on the constitutional parameter of rights review.
- **Amparo en Revisión 410/2012**: relevant to disability analysis and the social model approach.
- **Amparo Directo en Revisión 1387/2012**: relevant to psychosocial disability, guardianship, and legal capacity concerns.
- **Acción de Inconstitucionalidad 96/2014**: equality and non-discrimination as interpretive foundations.
- **Protocol for judicial decision-making in cases involving persons with disabilities** (SCJN, 2014): not binding jurisprudence, but an important interpretive and practical guide.

### Relevant Inter-American Court references

- **Ximenes Lopes v. Brazil (2006)**: psychosocial disability and standards of care.
- **Furlan and Family Members v. Argentina (2012)**: access to justice and disability-sensitive adjudication.
- **Artavia Murillo et al. v. Costa Rica (2012)**: autonomy, dignity, and life-planning considerations with broader interpretive relevance.
- **Gonzales Lluy et al. v. Ecuador (2015)**: intersectional discrimination involving health status, poverty, gender, and education.

### Analytical note for NormTrace

Precedents should not be inserted mechanically. Their relevance depends on the legal problem under review, the structure of the domestic legal instrument, and the type of right affected. The objective is not to accumulate citations, but to identify doctrinally relevant standards.

## 7. Template for an argument of unconventionality

The following template may be used to structure arguments for litigation, legislative advocacy, or rights-based legal analysis.

```text
ARGUMENT OF UNCONVENTIONALITY [NUMBER]

1. CHALLENGED OR ANALYSED NORM:
   [Article X of Law Y]

2. RIGHT AFFECTED:
   [Name of the right + article of the relevant international legal instrument]

3. CONTENT OF THE INTERNATIONAL STANDARD:
   [Paraphrase of the relevant treaty provision + interpretive guidance from a General Comment, case law, or concluding observation]
   Source: [instrument / General Comment / judgment / paragraph]

4. WAY IN WHICH THE DOMESTIC LEGAL INSTRUMENT FAILS TO MEET THE STANDARD:
   □ Total absence of the right
   □ Recognition without guarantee
   □ Regression from a prior standard
   □ Direct incompatibility
   □ Indirect discrimination
   □ Omission affecting a specific subgroup
   □ Implementation-oriented deficiency

   Explanation:
   [Concrete analysis]

5. RELEVANT PRECEDENT OR INTERPRETIVE MATERIAL:
   [Case / General Comment / observation / paragraph]

6. LEGAL CONSEQUENCE:
   [Possible domestic unconstitutionality, incompatibility with treaty obligations, discriminatory effect, or basis for international responsibility]

7. POSSIBLE REFORM OR REMEDIAL PATHWAY:
   Current wording: [...]
   Proposed wording: [...]
   Justification: [...]
