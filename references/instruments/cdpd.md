# Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)
## Reference Framework for Conventionality Analysis

**Adopted:** 13 December 2006 — UN General Assembly Resolution A/RES/61/106  
**Entry into force:** 3 May 2008  
**Mexico ratification / accession:** 17 December 2007  
**Optional Protocol ratified by Mexico:** 17 December 2007

---

## 1. General principles (Article 3)

Article 3 sets out the eight general principles that should guide the interpretation of the Convention and any assessment of whether a domestic legal instrument is aligned with it:

a) respect for inherent dignity, individual autonomy, including the freedom to make one’s own choices, and independence of persons;  
b) non-discrimination;  
c) full and effective participation and inclusion in society;  
d) respect for difference and acceptance of persons with disabilities as part of human diversity and humanity;  
e) equality of opportunity;  
f) accessibility;  
g) equality between men and women;  
h) respect for the evolving capacities of children with disabilities and respect for the right of children with disabilities to preserve their identities.

### Relevance for NormTrace

When assessing a domestic legal instrument, these principles should be treated as cross-cutting interpretive criteria. The question is not only whether a provision is formally consistent with the text of the Convention, but whether its legal effect is coherent with these general principles.

---

## 2. General obligations of States Parties (Article 4)

Under Article 4, States Parties undertake to:

- adopt all appropriate legislative, administrative, and other measures for the implementation of the rights recognised in the Convention;
- modify or abolish existing laws, regulations, customs, and practices that constitute discrimination against persons with disabilities;
- take into account the protection and promotion of the human rights of persons with disabilities in all policies and programmes;
- refrain from engaging in any act or practice that is inconsistent with the Convention;
- ensure that public authorities and institutions act in conformity with the Convention;
- take all appropriate measures to eliminate discrimination on the basis of disability by any person, organisation, or private enterprise;
- undertake or promote research and development of accessible goods, services, equipment, and facilities;
- provide accessible information about mobility aids, devices, and assistive technologies;
- promote the training of professionals and staff working with persons with disabilities in the rights recognised in the Convention.

### Relevance for NormTrace

For domestic legal analysis, Article 4 is especially important when asking whether a domestic legal framework:

- contains pathways for removing discriminatory provisions elsewhere in the legal order;
- extends relevant duties beyond public authorities to private actors where required;
- and creates operational conditions for implementation rather than merely declaratory commitments.

---

## 3. Women with disabilities (Article 6) — priority provision

Article 6 recognises that women and girls with disabilities are subject to **multiple discrimination** and requires States Parties to take measures to ensure their full and equal enjoyment of all human rights and fundamental freedoms.

It also requires States Parties to take all appropriate measures to ensure the **full development, advancement, and empowerment** of women for the purpose of guaranteeing the exercise and enjoyment of the rights set out in the Convention.

### General Comment No. 3 (2016)

General Comment No. 3 develops Article 6 and clarifies that States should, among other things:

- ensure that gender equality laws and anti-discrimination frameworks include disability;
- ensure that disability laws and policies incorporate gender;
- address violence against women and girls with disabilities in law and policy;
- and recognise women with disabilities as agents and rights-holders rather than passive recipients of assistance.

### Relevance for NormTrace

Article 6 should be used when assessing whether a domestic legal instrument:

- incorporates gender as a legally relevant dimension of disability;
- recognises multiple and intersectional discrimination;
- and avoids treating disability policy as gender-neutral where structural inequality suggests otherwise.

---

## 4. Children with disabilities (Article 7)

Article 7 requires States Parties to take all necessary measures to ensure the full enjoyment by children with disabilities of all human rights and fundamental freedoms on an equal basis with other children.

It further provides that:

- the **best interests of the child** shall be a primary consideration in all actions concerning children with disabilities;
- children with disabilities have the right to express their views freely on all matters affecting them;
- and those views must be given due weight according to age and maturity, on an equal basis with other children.

### Relevance for NormTrace

This provision is relevant when assessing whether domestic legal instruments:

- address disability only in adult terms;
- fail to provide child-specific guarantees;
- or omit participation and best-interests reasoning in matters concerning children with disabilities.

---

## 5. Accessibility (Article 9) — immediate and cross-cutting obligation

Article 9 requires States Parties to take appropriate measures to ensure access, on an equal basis with others, to:

- the physical environment;
- transportation;
- information and communications, including information and communications technologies and systems;
- and other facilities and services open or provided to the public,

in both **urban and rural areas**.

Such measures include:

- identifying and eliminating obstacles and barriers to accessibility;
- applying accessibility standards to buildings, roads, transport, schools, housing, medical facilities, and workplaces;
- providing signage in Braille and in easy-to-read and understandable forms;
- facilitating forms of live assistance and intermediaries, including guides, readers, and professional sign language interpreters;
- and promoting training for stakeholders involved in accessibility.

### General Comment No. 2 (2014)

General Comment No. 2 clarifies that accessibility is a **precondition for the exercise of rights** and an **ex ante duty**, not merely a case-by-case accommodation measure. It also makes clear that accessibility obligations are relevant in both public and private settings where goods, services, and facilities are open or provided to the public.

### Relevance for NormTrace

NormTrace should assess whether a domestic legal instrument treats accessibility as:

- an enforceable structural obligation;
- an immediate and cross-cutting duty;
- or merely a programme objective subject to indefinite delay.

---

## 6. Equal recognition before the law (Article 12) — legally central and often contested

Article 12 provides that:

1. persons with disabilities have the right to recognition everywhere as persons before the law;
2. persons with disabilities enjoy **legal capacity on an equal basis with others in all aspects of life**;
3. States Parties must provide access to the support persons with disabilities may require in exercising their legal capacity;
4. safeguards must be provided to prevent abuse in measures relating to the exercise of legal capacity.

### General Comment No. 1 (2014)

General Comment No. 1 interprets Article 12 as rejecting substituted decision-making regimes and requiring a transition towards **supported decision-making**.

This interpretation has generated significant judicial and academic debate, but it remains the Committee’s authoritative interpretive position.

### Relevance for Mexico

Article 12 is particularly important in the Mexican context when reviewing legal provisions on guardianship, interdiction, substituted decision-making, and civil capacity.

### Relevance for NormTrace

When analysing a domestic legal framework, the key question is whether it:

- recognises persons with disabilities as full legal actors;
- replaces or preserves substituted decision-making structures;
- and provides support mechanisms together with adequate safeguards.

---

## 7. Access to justice (Article 13)

Article 13 requires States Parties to ensure effective access to justice for persons with disabilities on an equal basis with others, including through **procedural and age-appropriate accommodations**.

It applies to all legal proceedings, whether civil, criminal, administrative, or other, and includes the participation of persons with disabilities as direct and indirect participants, including as witnesses.

States Parties must also promote appropriate training for those working in the administration of justice, including police and prison personnel.

### Relevance for NormTrace

A domestic legal instrument should be assessed not only for whether it formally recognises judicial access, but for whether it provides:

- procedural accommodation;
- accessibility in judicial settings;
- and duties of training and adaptation for justice institutions.

---

## 8. Liberty and security of the person (Article 14)

Article 14 provides that persons with disabilities, on an equal basis with others:

- enjoy the right to liberty and security of the person;
- shall not be deprived of their liberty unlawfully or arbitrarily;
- and that the existence of a disability shall in no case justify a deprivation of liberty.

### Relevance for NormTrace

This provision is especially relevant when assessing domestic legal instruments concerning:

- involuntary institutionalisation;
- compulsory treatment;
- mental health detention;
- or detention regimes that rely on disability status or diagnosis as a legal basis.

---

## 9. Inclusive education (Article 24)

Article 24 recognises the right of persons with disabilities to education and requires States Parties to ensure an **inclusive education system at all levels**.

Minimum standards include ensuring that persons with disabilities:

- are not excluded from the general education system on the basis of disability;
- have access to inclusive, quality, and free primary and secondary education on an equal basis with others in the communities in which they live;
- receive reasonable accommodation according to individual requirements;
- and obtain the support required within the general education system.

### General Comment No. 4 (2016)

General Comment No. 4 makes clear that inclusive education is the relevant standard and rejects segregated educational arrangements as a substitute for inclusion.

### Relevance for NormTrace

A domestic legal instrument should be assessed for whether it:

- protects inclusion as the baseline;
- preserves segregated structures as a default;
- or fails to create duties of accommodation and support within the general education system.

---

## 10. Work and employment (Article 27)

Article 27 recognises the right of persons with disabilities to work on an equal basis with others, including the opportunity to gain a living by work freely chosen or accepted in a labour market and work environment that are **open, inclusive, and accessible**.

States Parties are required, among other things, to:

- prohibit discrimination on the basis of disability in all matters concerning employment;
- protect the right to just and favourable conditions of work;
- ensure labour and trade union rights on an equal basis with others;
- provide access to technical and vocational guidance programmes;
- promote employment in the private sector through appropriate policies and measures, including affirmative action where relevant;
- and ensure that **reasonable accommodation** is provided in the workplace.

### General Comment No. 8 (2022)

General Comment No. 8 clarifies States Parties’ obligations concerning the right to work and employment under Article 27 and develops the Convention’s standards on open labour markets, non-discrimination, accessibility, accommodation, working conditions, and inclusion in employment.

### Relevance for NormTrace

Article 27 should be used to assess whether a domestic legal framework:

- prohibits disability discrimination effectively in employment;
- regulates duties of reasonable accommodation;
- and extends relevant obligations to private employers and labour-market institutions.

---

## 11. Adequate standard of living and social protection (Article 28)

Article 28 recognises the right of persons with disabilities to an adequate standard of living for themselves and their families, including adequate food, clothing, and housing, and to the continuous improvement of living conditions.

States Parties must take appropriate steps to safeguard and promote this right, including measures relating to:

- access to social protection programmes;
- access to public housing programmes;
- access to retirement benefits and social support;
- and equal access, especially for women and girls with disabilities, to poverty-reduction programmes.

### Relevance for NormTrace

This provision is useful when assessing whether domestic legal instruments:

- treat disability only as a matter of assistance rather than rights-based social protection;
- address poverty and disability in an integrated way;
- and create enforceable access pathways rather than discretionary benefits only.

---

## 12. Participation in political and public life (Article 29)

Article 29 requires States Parties to guarantee to persons with disabilities political rights and the opportunity to enjoy them on an equal basis with others.

This includes:

- the right to vote and to be elected;
- electoral procedures, facilities, and materials that are appropriate, accessible, and easy to understand and use;
- the right to vote by secret ballot where applicable;
- and participation in non-governmental organisations and associations concerned with public and political life.

### Relevance for NormTrace

A domestic legal framework should be assessed for whether it:

- guarantees political participation in practice;
- addresses accessibility in electoral procedures;
- and avoids capacity-based restrictions incompatible with equal political rights.

---

## 13. National implementation and monitoring framework (Articles 33–34)

### Article 33 — National implementation and monitoring

Article 33 requires States Parties to:

- designate one or more focal points within government for matters relating to implementation;
- give due consideration to the establishment of a coordination mechanism;
- and maintain, strengthen, designate, or establish an independent framework, including one or more independent mechanisms, as appropriate, to promote, protect, and monitor implementation.

It also requires the participation of civil society, in particular persons with disabilities and their representative organisations, in the monitoring process.

### Article 34 — Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities

Article 34 establishes the Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, which:

- examines periodic reports submitted by States Parties;
- issues concluding observations;
- and, through the Optional Protocol, may receive individual communications and conduct inquiries in accordance with its competence.

### Relevance for NormTrace

These provisions are particularly important when assessing whether a domestic legal framework:

- has a credible focal point and coordination mechanism;
- includes an adequately independent monitoring structure;
- and recognises the participatory role of civil society and representative organisations of persons with disabilities.

### Methodological note

This checklist is only indicative. Any meaningful conventionality analysis requires reading the full domestic legal instrument, the relevant implementing framework, and the applicable interpretive materials.
