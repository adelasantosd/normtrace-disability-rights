# Swiss Cantonal Framework
## Operational Framework for Cantonal Normative Analysis in NormTrace

This file provides the operational framework used by NormTrace to analyse **cantonal** domestic legal instruments within the Swiss legal order.

It should be read together with:

- `references/countries/switzerland/legal-system.md`

This file is not a substitute for the Swiss country module. Its purpose is narrower:

- to identify the relevant canton;
- to situate the instrument within the federal–cantonal legal structure;
- to assess whether the canton is acting within its sphere of competence;
- to determine whether federal law occupies the field, sets a framework, or leaves organisational discretion;
- and to evaluate the relevance of international human rights obligations, including the CRPD, at cantonal level.

---

## 1. Use rule within NormTrace

Use this file when the domestic legal instrument under review is:

- a cantonal constitution;
- a cantonal statute;
- a cantonal ordinance;
- a cantonal administrative rule;
- or a cantonal parliamentary or governmental explanatory document relevant to statutory interpretation.

Do **not** use this file as the primary reference for federal Swiss law.  
For federal legislation, start with:

- `references/countries/switzerland/legal-system.md`

## 2. Cantonal identification protocol

When a cantonal domestic legal instrument is submitted, proceed in the following sequence.

### Step A. Identify the canton

Look for:

- the explicit name of the canton;
- references to `Kanton`, `Canton`, or `Cantone`;
- the name of the cantonal parliament or executive;
- the language of the text;
- cantonal abbreviations where relevant;
- references to cantonal constitutions, official collections, or cantonal numbering systems.

### Step B. Identify the legal level of the instrument

Determine whether the text is:

- a cantonal constitutional provision;
- a cantonal statute;
- a cantonal ordinance or implementing regulation;
- a municipal or communal measure;
- or an explanatory or parliamentary document.

### Step C. Identify the subject matter

Determine whether the issue concerns:

- a field largely regulated by federal law;
- a field largely regulated by cantonal law;
- or a mixed field where federal law sets a framework and cantonal law implements or supplements it.

### Step D. Check for applicable federal law

Before analysing the cantonal text on its own, verify whether a relevant federal constitutional, statutory, or regulatory norm exists.

### Step E. Assess compatibility with higher law

If a federal norm exists, determine whether the cantonal norm:

- implements it;
- supplements it without contradiction;
- or conflicts with it.

### Step F. Assess international-law relevance

Where the issue concerns human rights or disability-related obligations, assess whether the relevant international norm:

- binds Switzerland;
- is relevant to cantonal action;
- may be directly applicable;
- or serves primarily as an interpretive and compatibility standard.

## 3. Constitutional principles governing the Confederation–canton relationship

### Article 3 BV — Residual sovereignty of the cantons

The cantons are sovereign except to the extent that their sovereignty is limited by the Federal Constitution. They exercise all rights that are not vested in the Confederation.

### Article 49 BV — Primacy of federal law

Federal law takes precedence over contrary cantonal law.

### Article 50 BV — Municipal autonomy

Municipal autonomy is guaranteed within the limits fixed by cantonal law.

### Relevance for NormTrace

These provisions mean that cantonal analysis must begin with three questions:

1. Has the Confederation been given competence in this subject matter?
2. If so, how far does federal regulation go?
3. If not, does the canton retain legislative or administrative space?

## 4. Working competence map for cantonal analysis

This section is a practical analytical guide. It is not an exhaustive statement of Swiss constitutional law.

### 4.1 Fields largely occupied by federal law

The following fields typically require caution because federal law plays a dominant role:

- civil law;
- criminal law;
- major areas of social insurance;
- migration and asylum;
- core labour-law structures;
- major transport and telecommunications frameworks.

### Analytical consequence

If a cantonal instrument appears to regulate one of these areas directly, the analyst should ask whether the canton is:

- acting within a delegated implementation role;
- supplementing without contradiction;
- or exceeding its competence.

### 4.2 Mixed or framework fields

The following fields often involve strong cantonal implementation or supplementation within a federal framework:

- education;
- health services;
- social support and welfare;
- accessibility in public services and buildings;
- cantonal and municipal procedural arrangements;
- political participation at cantonal and communal level;
- institutional care and support services.

### Analytical consequence

In these fields, the issue is often not whether the canton may legislate at all, but:

- whether it respects federal minimum requirements;
- whether it uses its implementation margin to strengthen or weaken rights protection;
- and whether it creates real institutional pathways for enforcement.

### 4.3 Predominantly cantonal organisational fields

Cantonal law is especially important in areas such as:

- cantonal administrative organisation;
- cantonal procedural arrangements;
- cantonal school systems;
- cantonal and communal service delivery;
- local accessibility implementation;
- and cantonal public-institution design.

### Analytical consequence

Where the canton retains organisational responsibility, cantonal legislation may be decisive for the practical enjoyment of rights, even if the underlying right is also protected by federal or international law.

## 5. Federal compatibility test for cantonal norms

When a cantonal norm is reviewed, apply the following sequence.

### Test 1. Does federal law exist in the subject matter?

- If **no**, the canton may retain broad competence under the logic of residual sovereignty.
- If **yes**, proceed to Test 2.

### Test 2. What kind of federal regulation exists?

Determine whether federal law:

- fully regulates the matter;
- sets a minimum or framework standard;
- or leaves organisational and implementation discretion to the cantons.

### Test 3. Is the cantonal norm contradictory?

Determine whether the cantonal norm is:

- directly contrary to federal law;
- merely different but compatible;
- more protective without contradiction;
- or organisationally distinct while remaining within the federal framework.

### Test 4. What is the likely legal consequence?

If the cantonal norm is contrary to federal law, it is vulnerable under the primacy rule of Article 49 BV.

If it supplements or concretises federal law without contradiction, it may remain valid.

### Relevance for NormTrace

This test is central because many cantonal disability-relevant rules do not fail by ignoring rights language, but by:

- acting outside competence;
- failing to implement a federal framework adequately;
- or using cantonal discretion in ways that weaken enforceability.

## 6. Cantonal legislative process: what to look for

Legislative structure varies across cantons, but certain recurring elements matter analytically.

### 6.1 Initiative stage

The analyst should identify whether the proposal came from:

- the cantonal government;
- a member or members of parliament;
- a parliamentary committee;
- or a cantonal popular initiative.

### 6.2 Pre-parliamentary and consultative materials

Where available, look for:

- consultation or pre-consultation documents;
- explanatory reports;
- committee reports;
- government messages;
- or equivalent preparatory materials.

These may reveal:

- whether disability organisations or other affected groups were consulted;
- whether compatibility with federal law was assessed;
- whether international standards were considered;
- and whether budgetary implications were explicitly addressed.

### 6.3 Parliamentary structure

Cantonal legislatures are unicameral. Their names and internal practices vary by canton.

For NormTrace, the key question is not the formal title of the parliament, but whether there is usable interpretive material showing:

- legislative intent;
- competence assumptions;
- rights reasoning;
- and institutional design choices.

### 6.4 Referendum environment

Swiss cantonal legislation operates in a direct-democratic environment. Depending on the canton, cantonal statutes may be subject to optional or mandatory referendum mechanisms.

### Relevance for NormTrace

This matters because cantonal drafting may be:

- more cautious;
- more consensus-oriented;
- more dependent on explanatory materials;
- and more sensitive to budgetary and administrative objections.

## 7. Cantonal messages, reports, and explanatory documents

For cantonal interpretation, documents equivalent to the federal *Botschaft* may be especially important.

These may appear under labels such as:

- `Botschaft`
- `Regierungsbotschaft`
- `rapport explicatif`
- `message du Conseil d’État`
- `Messaggio del Consiglio di Stato`
- committee report or similar parliamentary document

### What to look for

When available, ask:

- Does the document identify the constitutional basis of cantonal competence?
- Does it discuss compatibility with federal law?
- Does it mention the CRPD, equality guarantees, or other human rights instruments?
- Does it describe consultation with representative organisations?
- Does it assess costs, implementation structures, or cantonal administrative capacity?
- Does it adopt a medical, assistentialist, or rights-based model?

### Relevance for NormTrace

These materials can be essential when the domestic legal instrument is formally sparse or where rights-compatible interpretation depends on legislative intent or implementation assumptions.

## 8. International law at cantonal level

### 8.1 Cantonal obligation to respect international law

Switzerland follows a monist approach, and both the Confederation and the cantons must respect international law.

### 8.2 Analytical distinction: bindingness vs direct applicability

For cantonal analysis, always distinguish between:

- the fact that Switzerland is internationally bound;
- the fact that cantonal authorities are part of the state bound by that obligation;
- and the separate question whether a specific treaty norm is directly applicable before cantonal courts or administrative authorities.

### 8.3 CRPD relevance at cantonal level

The CRPD is relevant to cantonal action whenever the canton legislates or administers in fields such as:

- education;
- access to justice;
- public services;
- institutional care;
- participation;
- social support;
- and administrative procedures.

### 8.4 Practical use in NormTrace

At cantonal level, the CRPD may function as:

- a directly relevant legal standard where appropriate;
- a strong interpretive guide;
- a compatibility benchmark;
- and a diagnostic tool for identifying gaps in cantonal implementation.

### Caution

Do **not** assume automatically that every CRPD provision is directly self-executing in every cantonal context.  
That assessment requires issue-specific legal analysis.

## 9. Linguistic and cantonal diversity

Swiss cantonal analysis must remain sensitive to linguistic and institutional diversity.

### Linguistic relevance

A cantonal instrument may be drafted in:

- German;
- French;
- Italian;
- or, in specific contexts, Romansh.

Language can be analytically relevant because:

- the drafting style differs by linguistic region;
- interpretive nuance may vary between official versions where more than one language is legally authoritative;
- and accessibility questions may arise differently depending on the canton and the linguistic setting.

### Institutional diversity

The cantons do not all legislate, organise services, or document legislative intent in exactly the same way.

### Relevance for NormTrace

Do not assume that all cantons:

- use the same legislative form;
- produce the same volume of preparatory materials;
- or follow the same administrative logic.

The framework should therefore be applied comparatively but with caution.

## 10. Landsgemeinde note

Open popular assemblies (*Landsgemeinden*) continue to exist in some cantonal contexts, notably Glarus and Appenzell Innerrhoden.

### Relevance for NormTrace

Where a cantonal legislative or electoral process depends on a Landsgemeinde or another highly particular institutional form, the analyst should consider whether:

- participation barriers arise for persons with disabilities;
- records of legislative intent are limited;
- and accessibility concerns may become analytically relevant under participation and equality standards.

This note is not intended to predetermine the legal conclusion. It is a warning to look carefully at the institutional form of participation.

## 11. Notes for analysts using NormTrace in the cantonal Swiss context

When using NormTrace in relation to a cantonal Swiss domestic legal instrument, analysts should take particular care to:

- identify the canton correctly;
- identify the level of normativity correctly;
- verify whether federal law occupies or structures the field;
- distinguish contradiction from supplementation;
- distinguish bindingness of international law from direct applicability of a specific treaty provision;
- use cantonal preparatory materials where available;
- remain attentive to linguistic and institutional diversity;
- and avoid assuming that cantonal implementation is uniform across Switzerland.

In the Swiss cantonal context, legal adequacy often depends on the interaction between federal constitutional structure, federal statutory frameworks, cantonal implementation choices, municipal execution, and the practical domestic effect of international human rights standards.
