# Mexico Legal System Module
## Country Reference Framework for NormTrace

This document provides the country-specific legal reference framework used by NormTrace for context-sensitive normative analysis in Mexico. It is intended to support the pilot application on disability rights, while also serving as a reusable country module for future thematic extensions.

Its purpose is not to replace doctrinal legal research, but to provide the constitutional, institutional, and legislative context required to interpret domestic legal instruments in light of international standards.

---

## 1. Normative hierarchy in Mexico

Following the 2011 constitutional reform on human rights, legal analysis in Mexico must take into account not only the formal hierarchy of legal sources, but also the constitutional parameter of rights review shaped by Articles 1 and 133 of the Constitution and the interpretive framework developed by the Supreme Court.

A simplified working hierarchy for NormTrace is as follows:

```text
POLITICAL CONSTITUTION OF THE UNITED MEXICAN STATES (CPEUM)
  + International human rights treaties as part of the constitutional parameter of rights review
       ↓
INTERNATIONAL TREATIES IN OTHER SUBJECT AREAS
       ↓
CONSTITUTIONAL LAWS / GENERAL LAWS / FEDERAL LAWS / ORGANIC LAWS
       ↓
STATE LAWS (within their sphere of competence)
       ↓
FEDERAL REGULATIONS
       ↓
STATE AND MUNICIPAL REGULATIONS
       ↓
OFFICIAL MEXICAN STANDARDS (NOMs) / MEXICAN STANDARDS (NMXs)
       ↓
ADMINISTRATIVE AGREEMENTS / CIRCULARS

```
For analytical purposes, this means that domestic legislation should not be assessed in isolation. Laws must be read in light of the constitutional human rights framework and the applicable international standards.

Key reference: Supreme Court of Justice of the Nation, Contradicción de Tesis 293/2011; Articles 1 and 133 CPEUM.

## 2. Distribution of competences in the Mexican federal system

Mexico is a federal state with three constitutionally recognised orders of government:
	1.	Federal level: powers expressly assigned by the Constitution, especially under Article 73, together with recognised implied powers.
	2.	State level: residual powers, that is, those not expressly assigned to the federation or municipalities under Article 124.
	3.	Municipal level: constitutionally assigned local powers under Article 115, particularly in relation to local government, public services, and urban matters.

Relevance for rights analysis

In rights-based fields, the Mexican legal order often operates through a combination of exclusive, concurrent, and coordinated competences.

Examples include:
	•	Exclusive federal competences: foreign affairs, migration policy.
	•	Concurrent competences with a general law framework: education, health, disability, environment.
	•	Exclusive state competences: matters not expressly reserved to the federation or municipalities.
	•	Mandatory coordination schemes: areas organised through national systems, such as health or public security.

Analytical relevance for NormTrace

When analysing a general law, it is important to assess whether the norm:
	•	distributes powers clearly across the three orders of government,
	•	avoids leaving implementation responsibilities undefined,
	•	preserves the constitutional logic of federalism,
	•	and does not empty state or municipal competences of substantive content.

Diffuse or poorly allocated competences may signal implementation fragility even where rights are formally recognised.
