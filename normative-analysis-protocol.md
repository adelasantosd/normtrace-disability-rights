# NormTrace  
## Normative Analysis Protocol

This document sets out the core analytical protocol used by NormTrace for deep normative legal analysis.

The protocol is designed for the structured review of laws, decrees, regulations, and other legal instruments where the objective is not merely to identify textual matches, but to assess legal effect, institutional design, implicit exclusions, and alignment with international standards.

The current pilot application focuses on **disability rights in Mexico**, with an initial contrast between the **Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)** and domestic legislation.

## Analytical principle

NormTrace does not treat legal analysis as a keyword exercise.  
Its working premise is that the legal meaning of a norm depends not only on what the text declares, but also on:

- the actors it empowers or burdens,
- the remedies it creates or withholds,
- the institutions it activates or bypasses,
- the populations it includes or leaves unaddressed,
- and the practical enforceability of its guarantees.

Normative silence is treated as analytically relevant.

## Stage 0. Text ingestion and verification

Before analysis begins, obtain the full legal text under review.

Possible sources may include:

- uploaded files,
- pasted legal text,
- official legal databases,
- government gazettes,
- legislative portals,
- or institutional repositories.

The analyst should verify, where possible:

- whether the text is complete,
- whether it is the current version,
- and whether later reforms may affect interpretation.

Analysis based on partial or outdated text should be explicitly identified as such.

## Stage 1. Structural legal analysis

Review the internal architecture of the norm.

### 1.1 Formal anatomy
Identify:

- type of legal instrument,
- place in the domestic normative hierarchy,
- date of enactment and latest reform,
- structure by titles, chapters, sections, or articles.

### 1.2 Normative actor mapping
Identify the actors the norm assigns responsibilities to, including where relevant:

- executive authorities,
- legislative actors,
- judicial or quasi-judicial bodies,
- autonomous institutions,
- subnational authorities,
- private sector actors,
- civil society organisations,
- rights-holders themselves,
- and international bodies.

For each actor, identify:

- obligations,
- powers,
- implementation role,
- and any available mechanism of accountability or enforceability.

### 1.3 Responsibility flow
Assess whether the law:

- centralises responsibility,
- delegates downward,
- disperses duties without coordination,
- or creates a coherent intersectoral framework.

Where relevant, ask whether there is:

- a coordinating body,
- legal authority to act,
- budgetary support,
- sanctioning capacity,
- and institutional continuity.

### 1.4 Enforceability
Assess whether the norm includes:

- sanctions,
- judicial or administrative remedies,
- monitoring mechanisms,
- measurable targets,
- reporting duties,
- or other devices that move the law beyond declaration.

## Stage 2. Omissions and implicit exclusions

This stage examines what the law does not adequately articulate and the differentiated effects of that absence.

### 2.1 Subject of rights
Assess:

- who is explicitly recognised,
- how the legal subject is defined,
- whether the definition is restrictive or expansive,
- whether subgroups are left out implicitly,
- and whether the law adopts a rights-based or assistentialist construction.

### 2.2 Gender perspective
Review whether the norm:

- recognises gendered forms of disadvantage,
- addresses women and girls where relevant,
- accounts for gender-based violence,
- avoids exclusionary generic language,
- and anticipates differentiated legal effects.

### 2.3 Intersectionality
Examine whether the norm accounts for intersections with factors such as:

- indigeneity,
- migration status,
- age,
- poverty,
- rurality,
- sexual orientation,
- and gender identity.

### 2.4 Indigenous perspective and cultural accessibility
Where relevant, assess whether the norm:

- recognises indigenous peoples,
- incorporates consultation standards,
- accounts for cultural and linguistic accessibility,
- and avoids assuming a single homogeneous rights environment.

### 2.5 Language as exclusion
Review whether legal terminology:

- reflects outdated or pejorative concepts,
- medicalises the subject in ways that narrow rights,
- frames persons as passive beneficiaries rather than rights-holders,
- or otherwise reproduces subordinating assumptions.

## Stage 3. Conventionality and international alignment analysis

This stage compares the domestic legal framework with the relevant international instrument through legal function and effect rather than textual symmetry.

### 3.1 Applicable standard
Identify the applicable international and domestic interpretive framework, which may include:

- treaty provisions,
- constitutional clauses,
- relevant general comments,
- regional human rights standards,
- and authoritative interpretive criteria.

### 3.2 Functional contrast
Assess whether the domestic norm is consistent with principles such as:

- non-regression,
- progressivity,
- maximum protection,
- effectiveness,
- and non-discrimination.

### 3.3 Gap identification
Classify findings where relevant as:

- total absence,
- weak or declaratory recognition,
- incompatibility,
- regression,
- or implementation-oriented legal gap.

The objective is not merely to record legal difference, but to evaluate the legal significance of that difference.

## Stage 4. Structured legal argumentation

Translate findings into structured legal arguments.

Each argument should identify:

- the core finding,
- the legal standard engaged,
- the type of deficiency,
- supporting doctrinal or interpretive material,
- and a possible line of reform, remedy, or advocacy.

Arguments may be organised according to urgency, remediability, and likely forum of use.

## Stage 5. Output production

Depending on the use case, the protocol may support outputs such as:

- legal diagnostic notes,
- comparative tables,
- legislative reform briefs,
- advocacy documents,
- and structured argument sets for litigation support.

## Adaptation to other countries or domains

The protocol is modular.  
To extend it to other jurisdictions or fields, the analyst should incorporate:

- country-specific legal hierarchy,
- relevant constitutional and treaty framework,
- institutional enforcement context,
- and sector-specific international instruments.

The current repository documents the **Mexico disability-rights pilot**, but the protocol is designed for future extension to broader rights and governance domains.

## Methodological cautions

1. Legal analysis should never be reduced to textual coincidence alone.  
2. Normative silence must be treated as analytically relevant.  
3. Primary legal sources should always be prioritised.  
4. Diagnostic analysis and reform recommendation should remain analytically distinct.
