# NormTrace

**NormTrace** is a repository-based, AI-assisted analytical system for examining how **domestic legal frameworks** and **domestic legal instruments** align with relevant **international legal instruments** and **international standards**.

This repository documents the **pilot phase** of the project, currently focused on **disability rights** through a comparative and multilevel architecture centred on:

- **Mexico**
- **Switzerland** (federal and cantonal levels)
- the **Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)**

NormTrace is designed as a **documented, citable, and methodologically transparent pilot**, not as a complete public release of every production component of any underlying application environment.

---

## Overview

NormTrace was developed to move beyond purely textual legal review. Rather than relying on keyword matching or superficial article-by-article comparison, it applies a **structured, multi-layered legal methodology** designed to assess:

- legal effect;
- institutional design;
- implicit exclusions;
- normative alignment;
- legal compatibility with international standards;
- and implementation-oriented gaps.

Its central methodological premise is that a domestic legal instrument cannot be assessed solely by what it declares. It must also be assessed by what it enables, omits, distributes, and makes practically claimable.

In this approach, **normative silence is analytically meaningful**.  
Absence, ambiguity, under-specification, weak coordination, and limited enforceability are treated as legally relevant features rather than neutral drafting gaps.

---

## What NormTrace is

NormTrace is a **structured methodological and documentary system** for deep normative legal analysis.

It supports analysis through a reproducible sequence of interpretive steps, assisted by:

- analytical frameworks;
- country-specific legal context modules;
- international legal instrument files;
- rapid operational reference files;
- case-law notes;
- and controlled terminology.

The system is intended for uses such as:

- legislative review;
- strategic litigation support;
- policy reform analysis;
- international human rights compatibility analysis;
- conventionality analysis where jurisdictionally appropriate;
- institutional review;
- and comparative or mixed-method research.

---

## What NormTrace is not

NormTrace is **not**:

- a substitute for expert legal judgment;
- an automated constitutional court;
- a real-time jurisprudential database;
- a direct measure of behavioural compliance;
- or a self-sufficient source of implementation evidence.

At this stage, the system assesses primarily:

- **normative alignment**;
- **legal compatibility**;
- **institutional anchoring**;
- and **implementation-oriented gaps**.

Its outputs are analytical and diagnostic. They must be verified against primary legal sources and, where relevant, complemented with empirical evidence on implementation, enforcement, budget, administration, and lived experience.

## Current pilot scope

### Jurisdictions
- **Mexico**
- **Switzerland**

### Substantive area
- **Disability rights**

### Core international legal instrument
- **Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)**

### Current domestic pilot focus
- **Mexico**: domestic disability-related legal framework, including the *Ley General para la Inclusión de las Personas con Discapacidad* (LGIPD)
- **Switzerland**: federal disability-equality framework, including the *Behindertengleichstellungsgesetz* (BehiG / LHand / LDis), with attention to cantonal implementation contexts

This pilot was selected because it offers:

- a strong international normative benchmark;
- a rich doctrinal field;
- and an analytically useful comparison between two jurisdictions with markedly different constitutional structures, review pathways, and subnational implementation logics.

## How the method works

NormTrace follows five main analytical stages:

1. **Text ingestion and verification**  
   Obtain and verify the domestic legal instrument under review.

2. **Structural analysis**  
   Assess the formal anatomy, institutional architecture, competence context, and enforceability structure of the domestic legal instrument.

3. **Exclusion and omission analysis**  
   Identify direct, indirect, structural, and omission-based forms of exclusion, including gendered, intersectional, and culturally or territorially differentiated barriers where relevant.

4. **Conventionality or international compatibility analysis**  
   Compare the domestic legal framework with the relevant international legal instrument through legal function and effect rather than textual symmetry alone.

5. **Structured legal argumentation**  
   Translate findings into arguments suitable for litigation, legislative advocacy, institutional review, or research use.

---

## Jurisdiction-sensitive analytical logic

NormTrace does not assume that all jurisdictions process international law in the same way.

For example:

- in **Mexico**, analysis may proceed through the logic of **conventionality control** within the **constitutional parameter of rights review**;
- in **Switzerland**, the functionally comparable inquiry is more precisely framed as **international human rights compatibility analysis**, taking into account monism, direct applicability, federal structure, and cantonal implementation.

This jurisdiction-sensitive design is central to the repository’s methodology.

---

## Scalability

Although the current repository documents a disability-rights pilot centred on Mexico and Switzerland, the analytical architecture is modular and extensible.

In principle, the same structure can be adapted to:

- other countries;
- other legal traditions;
- other international legal instruments;
- and other rights-based or governance-related domains.

Country modules, instrument files, rapid-reference files, and case-law notes are part of this design. They make it possible to adapt the methodology across jurisdictions while preserving legal specificity.

---

## Quantitative and mixed-method potential

NormTrace is primarily an interpretive legal system. However, its outputs can also be structured in a way that supports later comparative, quantitative, or mixed-method work.

In particular, the protocol is designed to generate coding-ready summaries of:

- type of gap;
- degree of enforceability;
- responsible actor;
- subgroup affected;
- participation and monitoring mechanisms;
- and overall normative alignment.

These structured outputs are intended to support later dataset construction and comparative analysis, not to replace legal interpretation.

---

## Limitations

NormTrace has important limitations.

It does not replace legal advice or case-specific litigation strategy.

It does not independently guarantee that the legal text under review reflects the most recent reform unless the source provided is current and has been checked.

It does not produce empirical evidence on implementation or outcomes on its own.

It does not collapse the distinction between:

- treaty text;
- judicial precedent;
- General Comments;
- concluding observations;
- and doctrinal guidance.

Its findings should therefore be understood as part of a broader analytical process that may also require jurisprudential review, institutional evidence, budget analysis, and qualitative or quantitative implementation data.
