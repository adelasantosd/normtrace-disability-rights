# NormTrace
## Methodological Note
**Version 2.0 — 2026**  
**Current pilot jurisdictions:** Mexico; Switzerland  
**Current pilot field:** Disability rights  
**Core international legal instrument:** Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)


## 1. Introduction and purpose

NormTrace is a repository-based, AI-assisted analytical system for multilevel normative analysis. It was developed to move beyond purely textual approaches to legislative review, such as keyword searching or formal article-by-article comparison, and to support a more rigorous assessment of how domestic legal frameworks and domestic legal instruments align with relevant international legal instruments and international standards.

The current pilot focuses on disability rights in Mexico and Switzerland, using the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) as the primary international benchmark. In Mexico, the pilot engages the domestic disability-related legal framework with particular reference to the LGIPD. In Switzerland, it engages the federal disability-equality framework, including the BehiG/LHand/LDis, while also recognising the analytical relevance of cantonal implementation.

The central methodological premise is that a domestic legal instrument cannot be assessed solely by what it declares. Legal analysis must also consider what the norm enables, omits, obscures, distributes, and renders enforceable. A norm is not only a textual artefact; it is also an institutional arrangement that shapes rights, duties, access, remedies, participation, and visibility.

From this perspective, **normative silence is analytically meaningful**. Omissions, ambiguities, under-specification, and weak institutional design are not incidental drafting issues alone; they may form part of the legal architecture through which rights are diluted, deferred, or rendered difficult to claim in practice.

NormTrace is designed for uses such as legislative review, strategic litigation support, conventionality analysis, norm-monitoring, academic research, advocacy, and policy reform analysis.

## 2. Analytical problem

In many legal and policy fields, the gap between international commitment and domestic implementation is not produced only by overt legal contradiction. It often emerges through weaker and less visible mechanisms: incomplete transposition, diffuse institutional responsibility, declaratory rights without guarantees, inaccessible remedies, absence of sanctions, weak coordination, or the exclusion of specific groups from the practical reach of a domestic legal instrument.

This problem is especially relevant in rights-based governance. Ratification of an international legal instrument does not by itself ensure **institutional anchoring**, operational coherence, or enforceability within the **domestic legal framework**. A domestic legal framework may appear aligned at the level of rhetoric while remaining partial, fragmented, or institutionally ineffective.

NormTrace addresses this problem by structuring legal analysis around the relationship between **international standards**, **domestic legal design**, and **implementation-oriented legal capacity**.

## 3. Theoretical and doctrinal foundations

The pilot methodology draws on five principal analytical traditions.

### 3.1 International human rights law

NormTrace is grounded in the doctrinal architecture of international human rights law, especially the understanding that states assume obligations to **respect, protect, and fulfil** rights, and that domestic law should be interpreted and applied according to the most protective standard available where relevant.

The tool therefore treats domestic legal analysis as inseparable from the international legal instruments that bind or orient state action.

### 3.2 Conventionality analysis and international human rights compatibility analysis

NormTrace uses a jurisdiction-sensitive approach to the relationship between domestic law and international standards.

In the Mexican context, the methodology is informed by the logic of conventionality analysis within the constitutional parameter of rights review, especially after the 2011 constitutional reform on human rights.

In the Swiss context, the functionally comparable inquiry is better framed as international human rights compatibility analysis. There, the relevant questions concern monist incorporation, direct applicability, federal structure, cantonal implementation, and the interaction between domestic constitutional guarantees, federal statutory law, and international human rights obligations.

For the purposes of NormTrace, neither of these approaches is reduced to simple textual comparison. In both cases, the task is to assess whether the domestic legal framework gives adequate legal effect to the relevant international standard.

### 3.3 Social model of disability

The disability-rights pilot adopts the **social model of disability** as its substantive baseline. This matters methodologically because it shifts the analytical focus away from individual impairment and towards the legal, institutional, social, and environmental barriers that restrict equal participation.

Under this model, a domestic legal instrument may be formally inclusive while still reproducing exclusion if it relies on assistentialist categories, passive beneficiary language, inaccessible procedures, or weak guarantees.

### 3.4 Gender and intersectionality

NormTrace assumes that formally neutral legal language may conceal differentiated effects. For this reason, the methodology includes a structured review of gender, intersectionality, and other cross-cutting dimensions of exclusion.

This involves examining whether a domestic legal instrument accounts for compounded disadvantage associated with factors such as age, indigeneity, migration status, poverty, rurality, sexual orientation, gender identity, and other relevant forms of structural disadvantage.

### 3.5 Indigenous rights and legal pluralism

In contexts such as Mexico, legal analysis cannot be limited to a single formal normative order. NormTrace therefore incorporates a perspective attentive to indigenous peoples’ rights, consultation standards, cultural and linguistic accessibility, and the coexistence of plural normative frameworks.

This does not imply collapsing all normative systems into one analytical plane. Rather, it recognises that domestic legal adequacy may depend on whether a norm is intelligible, reachable, and legally usable across diverse legal and cultural contexts.

## 4. What NormTrace is and what it is not

NormTrace is a repository-based, AI-assisted analytical system that guides legal analysis through a reproducible sequence of interpretive steps supported by reference frameworks, country modules, instrument files, case-law notes, and primary legal materials.

It is not:

- a substitute for expert legal judgment;
- an automated constitutional court;
- a real-time jurisprudential database;
- or a direct measure of behavioural compliance.

At this stage, the tool assesses primarily:

- **normative alignment**;
- **legal compatibility** with international standards;
- **institutional anchoring**;
- and **implementation-oriented gaps**.

Its outputs are analytical and diagnostic. They require verification against primary legal sources and, where appropriate, should be complemented by empirical evidence on implementation, enforcement, and lived experience.

## 5. Methodological structure

The NormTrace pilot is organised into five stages.

### Stage 0. Text ingestion and verification

The domestic legal instrument under review must first be obtained, verified, and checked for completeness and currency. Analysis should be based on the full and current version of the instrument wherever possible.

### Stage 1. Structural analysis

This stage identifies the anatomy of the domestic legal instrument and maps its institutional architecture. It examines:

- type and hierarchy of the domestic legal instrument;
- internal organisation of the norm;
- distribution of powers and duties;
- actors charged with implementation;
- coordination mechanisms;
- accountability provisions;
- sanctions, remedies, indicators, monitoring mechanisms, and implementation pathways.

The purpose is to determine whether the domestic legal instrument creates a coherent and enforceable structure rather than a merely declaratory framework.

### Stage 2. Exclusion and omission analysis

This stage focuses on what the domestic legal instrument omits and on the differentiated legal effects of those omissions. It examines:

- how the subject of rights is defined;
- whether the instrument reproduces restrictive or medicalised categories;
- whether it incorporates gender perspective;
- whether it recognises intersectional forms of exclusion;
- whether indigenous and culturally specific realities are considered;
- whether legal language itself functions as a mechanism of exclusion;
- and whether normative silence generates subgroup-specific barriers.

### Stage 3. Conventionality and international alignment analysis

The domestic legal framework is assessed against the applicable international legal instrument through a functional legal comparison. The methodology focuses on legal effect rather than textual symmetry.

The analysis asks whether the domestic legal instrument reflects the relevant international standard in terms of:

- non-regression;
- progressivity;
- maximum protection;
- effectiveness;
- equality;
- and non-discrimination.

This stage produces a structured mapping of legal gaps, including absence, weak recognition, incompatibility, regression, structural exclusion, and implementation-oriented deficiencies.

### Stage 4. Structured legal argumentation

Findings are translated into structured arguments identifying:

- the relevant legal problem;
- the applicable domestic or international standard;
- the type of deficiency;
- supporting doctrine or interpretive material;
- and a possible line of reform, remedy, litigation, or advocacy.

### Stage 5. Output production

The methodology may generate outputs in different formats depending on use:

- legal diagnostics;
- treaty–law comparison tables;
- reform briefs;
- structured argument sets for litigation or advocacy;
- institutional review notes;
- and coding-ready analytical summaries for later comparative or mixed-method work.

## 6. Current pilot scope

The present pilot is limited to:

- **jurisdictions:** Mexico; Switzerland
- **substantive area:** disability rights
- **core international legal instrument:** CRPD
- **current domestic pilot focus:** Mexican disability-related legal framework, especially the LGIPD; Swiss disability-equality framework, especially the BehiG/LHand/LDis, with cantonal relevance where implementation is subnational

This focus was chosen because it offers a strong international benchmark, a rich doctrinal field, and a particularly useful comparison between jurisdictions with different constitutional structures, review pathways, and subnational implementation logics.

## 7. Scalability

Although the current repository documents a disability-rights pilot centred on Mexico and Switzerland, the underlying architecture is modular and extensible.

In principle, the same analytical structure may be adapted to:

- other countries;
- other legal traditions;
- other international legal instruments;
- and other domains where implementation depends on domestic legal anchoring.

This includes, but is not limited to, health governance, social rights, gender equality, indigenous rights, and other rights-based or treaty-linked areas of law and policy.

The repository is also designed to support later comparative, quantitative, or mixed-method work through structured outputs that remain subordinate to legal interpretation.

## 8. Epistemic stance

NormTrace makes its interpretive premises explicit. It is grounded in binding international human rights standards and in doctrinal frameworks that treat omissions, exclusionary effects, and weak enforceability as legally relevant features of domestic legislation.

It does not claim neutrality between all possible readings of a norm where applicable legal standards exist. Its objective is to make visible the distance between domestic legal design and the relevant international standard in a transparent, structured, and verifiable way.

## 9. Limitations

NormTrace has important limitations.

It does not replace legal advice or case-specific litigation strategy.

It does not independently guarantee that the legal text under review reflects the most recent reform unless the source provided is current.

It does not produce empirical evidence on actual enforcement or lived outcomes on its own.

Its findings should therefore be understood as part of a broader analytical process that may also require jurisprudential review, institutional evidence, budget analysis, and qualitative or quantitative implementation data.

It may support later coding and comparison, but such structured outputs do not replace interpretive legal analysis.
