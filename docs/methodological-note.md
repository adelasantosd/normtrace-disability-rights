# NormTrace  
## Methodological Note  
**Version 1.0 — March 2025**  
**Initial scope:** Mexico  
**Pilot case:** CRPD vs. LGIPD

## 1. Introduction and purpose

NormTrace is a structured methodological tool for deep normative legal analysis. It was developed to move beyond purely textual approaches to legislative review, such as keyword searching or formal article-by-article comparison, and to support a more rigorous assessment of how domestic legal frameworks align with international legal instruments.

The current pilot focuses on disability rights in Mexico, using an initial contrast between the **Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)** and the **Ley General para la Inclusión de las Personas con Discapacidad (LGIPD)**. This pilot serves as a proof of concept for a broader analytical framework that may later be extended to other jurisdictions, policy domains, and international commitments.

The central methodological premise is that a law cannot be assessed solely by what it declares. Legal analysis must also consider what the norm enables, omits, obscures, distributes, and renders enforceable. A norm is not only a textual artefact; it is also an institutional arrangement that shapes rights, duties, access, remedies, and visibility.

From this perspective, **normative silence is analytically meaningful**. Omissions, ambiguities, under-specification, and weak institutional design are not incidental drafting issues alone; they may form part of the legal architecture through which rights are diluted, deferred, or rendered difficult to claim in practice.

NormTrace was designed for uses such as legislative review, strategic litigation support, norm-monitoring, academic research, advocacy, and policy reform analysis.

## 2. Analytical problem

In many legal and policy fields, the gap between international commitment and domestic implementation is not produced only by overt legal contradiction. It often emerges through weaker and less visible mechanisms: incomplete transposition, diffuse institutional responsibility, declaratory rights without guarantees, inaccessible remedies, absence of sanctions, or the exclusion of specific groups from the practical reach of a law.

This problem is especially relevant in rights-based governance. Ratification of an international treaty does not by itself ensure domestic legal anchoring, operational coherence, or enforceability. A domestic legal framework may appear compliant at the level of rhetoric while remaining partial, fragmented, or institutionally ineffective.

NormTrace addresses this problem by structuring legal analysis around the relationship between **international standards**, **domestic legal design**, and **implementation-oriented legal capacity**.

## 3. Theoretical and doctrinal foundations

The pilot methodology draws on five principal analytical traditions.

### 3.1 International human rights law

NormTrace is grounded in the doctrinal architecture of international human rights law, especially the understanding that states assume obligations to **respect, protect, and fulfil** rights, and that domestic law should be interpreted and applied according to the most protective standard available where relevant.

The tool therefore treats domestic legal analysis as inseparable from the international legal instruments that bind or orient state action.

### 3.2 Conventionality control and domestic legal review

In the Mexican context, the methodology is informed by the logic of **control de convencionalidad**, particularly after the 2011 constitutional reform on human rights. This implies that domestic norms should be assessed not only against constitutional text, but also in light of applicable international human rights treaties and their authoritative interpretation.

For the purposes of this pilot, conventionality analysis does not mean simple textual comparison. It means evaluating whether the domestic law gives adequate legal effect to the standard established by the relevant international instrument.

### 3.3 Social model of disability

The disability-rights pilot adopts the **social model of disability** as its substantive baseline. This matters methodologically because it shifts the analytical focus away from individual impairment and toward the legal, institutional, social, and environmental barriers that restrict equal participation.

Under this model, a law may be formally inclusive while still reproducing exclusion if it relies on assistentialist categories, passive beneficiary language, inaccessible procedures, or weak guarantees.

### 3.4 Gender and intersectionality

NormTrace assumes that formally neutral legal language may conceal differentiated effects. For this reason, the methodology includes a structured review of gender, intersectionality, and other cross-cutting dimensions of exclusion.

This involves examining whether the law accounts for compounded disadvantage associated with factors such as age, indigeneity, migration status, poverty, rurality, sexual orientation, and gender identity.

### 3.5 Indigenous rights and legal pluralism

In contexts such as Mexico, legal analysis cannot be limited to a single formal normative order. NormTrace therefore incorporates a perspective attentive to indigenous peoples’ rights, consultation standards, cultural and linguistic accessibility, and the coexistence of plural normative frameworks.

This does not imply collapsing all normative systems into one analytical plane, but rather recognising that domestic legal adequacy may depend on whether a norm is intelligible and accessible across diverse legal and cultural contexts.

## 4. What NormTrace is and what it is not

NormTrace is a **structured methodological tool** that guides legal analysis through a reproducible sequence of interpretive steps supported by reference frameworks and primary legal materials.

It is not:

- a substitute for expert legal judgment,
- an automated constitutional court,
- a real-time jurisprudential database,
- or a direct measure of behavioural compliance.

At this stage, the tool assesses primarily:

- domestic normative alignment,
- legal compatibility with international standards,
- institutional anchoring,
- and implementation-oriented legal gaps.

Its outputs are analytical and diagnostic. They require verification against primary legal sources and, where appropriate, should be complemented by empirical evidence on implementation, enforcement, and lived experience.

## 5. Methodological structure

The NormTrace pilot is organised into five stages.

### Stage 0. Text ingestion and verification

The legal text under review must first be obtained, verified, and checked for completeness and currency. Analysis should be based on the full and current version of the instrument wherever possible.

### Stage 1. Structural legal analysis

This stage identifies the anatomy of the norm and maps its institutional architecture. It examines:

- type and hierarchy of the legal instrument,
- internal organisation of the norm,
- distribution of powers and duties,
- actors charged with implementation,
- coordination mechanisms,
- accountability provisions,
- sanctions, remedies, indicators, and monitoring mechanisms.

The purpose is to determine whether the law creates a coherent and enforceable structure rather than a merely declaratory framework.

### Stage 2. Omissions and implicit exclusions

This stage focuses on what the law omits and on the differentiated legal effects of those omissions. It examines:

- how the subject of rights is defined,
- whether the law reproduces restrictive or medicalised categories,
- whether it integrates gender perspective,
- whether it recognises intersectional forms of exclusion,
- whether indigenous and culturally specific realities are considered,
- and whether legal language itself functions as a mechanism of exclusion.

### Stage 3. Conventionality and international alignment analysis

The domestic norm is assessed against the applicable international instrument through a functional legal comparison. The methodology focuses on legal effect rather than textual symmetry.

The analysis asks whether the domestic law reflects the relevant standard in terms of:

- non-regression,
- progressivity,
- maximum protection,
- and effectiveness.

This stage produces a structured mapping of legal gaps, including absence, weak recognition, incompatibility, regression, and implementation-related deficiencies.

### Stage 4. Structured legal argumentation

Findings are translated into structured arguments identifying:

- the relevant legal problem,
- the applicable domestic or international standard,
- the type of deficiency,
- supporting doctrine or interpretive material,
- and a possible line of reform, remedy, or advocacy.

### Stage 5. Output production

The methodology may generate outputs in different formats depending on use:

- legal diagnostics,
- treaty–law comparison tables,
- reform briefs,
- structured argument sets for litigation or advocacy,
- and implementation-oriented legislative notes.

## 6. Pilot scope

The present pilot is limited to:

- **jurisdiction:** Mexico,
- **substantive area:** disability rights,
- **initial contrast:** CRPD vs. LGIPD.

This focus was chosen because it offers a strong international benchmark, a rich doctrinal field, and a clear opportunity to test the methodology against a legal domain where language, institutional design, accessibility, and enforceability are particularly important.

## 7. Scalability

Although the current repository documents a pilot for disability rights in Mexico, the underlying architecture is modular and extensible.

In principle, the same analytical structure may be adapted to:

- other countries,
- other legal traditions,
- other international instruments,
- and other domains where implementation depends on domestic legal anchoring.

This includes, but is not limited to, health governance, social rights, gender equality, indigenous rights, and other rights-based or treaty-linked areas of law and policy.

## 8. Epistemic stance

NormTrace makes its interpretive premises explicit. It is grounded in binding international human rights standards and in doctrinal frameworks that treat omissions, exclusionary effects, and weak enforceability as legally relevant features of domestic legislation.

It does not claim neutrality between all possible readings of a norm when applicable legal standards exist. Its objective is to make visible the distance between domestic legal design and the relevant standard in a transparent, structured, and verifiable way.

## 9. Limitations

NormTrace has important limitations.

It does not replace legal advice or case-specific litigation strategy.

It does not independently guarantee that the legal text under review reflects the most recent reform unless the source provided is current.

It does not produce empirical evidence on actual enforcement or lived outcomes on its own.

Its findings should therefore be understood as part of a broader analytical process that may also require jurisprudential review, institutional evidence, budget analysis, and qualitative or quantitative implementation data.
